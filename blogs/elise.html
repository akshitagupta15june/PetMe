Who names a dog Darryl?

Elise Richardsson

October 16, 2022

A story about learning to ‘dog’ again.

I’ve been fostering dogs with my local humane society for the last 20 years, and my first ‘foster fail’ happened 2 years ago with a dog named Darryl.

Darryl first came to me as a foster dog 5 years ago as a nervous, tanned labrador mix who barked wildly from his (unlocked) kennel when anyone came into the house. He seemed intimidating, but he was more scared than anything else.

Darryl’s history was unknown to the humane society but his fearful personality was unpredictable and caused him to be very withdrawn, so my first few weeks with him were simply to let him acclimate to the space and observe me. Our interactions were strictly transactional and, by choice, he would stay in his kennel most of the day, even to eat. I didn’t attempt to sooth or comfort him in any way, nor did I try to excite him or entice play right away, in fact there were no strong emotions of any kind around him. I needed him to see me as a strong and stable person he could trust before anything else, so this is what I focused on.

After a few weeks he was comfortable enough to come out for meals and even gave me a tail wag when I picked his leash up and called him for a walk.A few more weeks after that I started letting him socialise with my two cats, and while he seemed unsure of them most days (whining in confusion when Fifi, all 7lbs of her, cozied up in his bed while he was busy eating) he seemed content to share a space with them and ignored them most of the time.

Fast forward 8 months and Darryl and I became very good companions, he learned to trust me, which gave him some confidence and I was able to curb his nerves and help him become a bit more brave in the loud and busy world. Darryl had made significant progress and I felt he was finally ready to meet some people to hopefully find his forever home. I stressed to the adoption counsellors that he needed to be in a home with no children as their unpredictability would likely trigger his own and he needed to be with people who would be strong and confident for him, until he felt safe enough to be strong and confident with them as well.

About a month later they had found someone who wanted to adopt Darryl. No children at home, check! Strong and confident woman with previous dog owner experience, check! Things looked very good, so off Darryl went to his new forever home :)

Except, not really.

Jump to three years later and Darryl’s new owner had brought him back to the humane society after he bit her in the face. The circumstances were confusing, she knelt down over his head to put some medication on his skin and he bit her, seemingly out of nowhere. The humane society reached out to me and of course I would be willing to help! However seeing Darryl again for the first time was quite heartbreaking, he was not the newly confident dog I had taken care of 3 years earlier.

By the time I was able to get to him he had been at the humane society for just over 24 hours, he hadn’t eaten and would not come out of his kennel to go for a bathroom break. He would bark at the staff when they tried to approach him and because he now had a bite history, they were even more cautious around him. I approached his kennel slowly, not making eye contact. He seemed to remember me and perked his head and did not bark when I approached. He got up, gave me a small tail wag and walked over cautiously. After a few minutes of just sitting with each other, I was able to leash him and we went outside for a short walk in the yard. He came home with me that night.

This version of Darryl was quite different. Not nervous exactly, but almost absent, like he wasn’t really there most of the time. He was on some pretty strong anti-anxiety medication and didn’t really seem interested in anything the same way he had been when he stayed with me last. Previously, he had slowly transformed into a happy pooch whose confidence was growing everyday and lots of things excited him, but not now. Now he seemed to be disinterested in everything.

It was unclear why he had been put on the medication in the first place, and I knew he hadn’t needed them last time so working with the humane society veterinarian team we decided to wean him off them in the hopes of awakening the dog I knew before. I was pretty confident I could ‘teach him how to dog again’, as a friend put it.

3 months of weaning him off the pills and he was starting to come around again, excited by cheese treats and whining at Fifi when she would steal his bed. He was looking like a real dog again, and that’s when I decided that I wanted him to be MY real dog. He was coming out of his shell again and I was falling for him in a real way. Another month of deciding and I signed the papers to make me his forever home.

And then, Thanksgiving happened.

Canadian Thanksgiving, kind of like American Thanksgiving minus the football; 3 days with family, way too much food, and this year, a double whammy heartbreak.

My mom’s dog Trixie had been ill all weekend, having trouble breathing and not eating for almost 48 hours. Back and forth to the vet the whole week before and then Monday morning we knew it was her time. We took her.

Because Darryl was still learning how to ‘dog’ again, and we were still working on his confidence and his unpredictability around those he was nervous about, he and I stayed in the basement of my mom’s house away from the busy-ness of the weekend and the world that Darryl was just starting to get used to again. I had been sad and crying about the loss of Trixie and without thinking about what I was doing, went to Darryl for a hug. However Darryl was very confused and unsure of my sad and seemingly unstable emotions and reacted to the lack of strength he was seeing with an alligator bite at my face.

Chomp, chomp, chomp, chomp.

I wish I could say he just missed me by a millimetre, however he did not, he got me right in the mouth and then on the wrist after I grabbed at his collar to try and protect myself. Blood poured down my chin while I quickly put some distance between us and this time I was off to the human emergency room.

9 hours and a minor reconstructive procedure later and I was back at my mom’s house swollen and the second heartbreak of the weekend (after losing Trixie) hit me, what to do with Darryl. Oh Darryl, we had come so far, what had gone wrong? Where do we go from here? Who names a dog Darryl anyway?

The long day at emerge was spent talking to my dog trainer friends about what had happened. And we came to a conclusion, Darryl was still learning ‘how to dog’ again, he needed me to be strong and confident because he couldn’t be, he didn’t know how. I needed to be his rock so he could trust that he would be safe and taken care of with me and only then could he learn to build his own confidence. It was the same story as when he came to me the first time as a foster, but this time I had shown him something that he hadn’t seen from me before. My grief for Trixie. My sadness had surfaced as unstable and chaotic emotions/energy that made him feel unsure and unsafe and I forced him into a hug where he was surrounded by it, so he did the one thing he knew how to do, he tried to protect himself.

A few days later and I’m starting anew with Darryl for a third time. He’s taken a small step back in his progress, still seemingly unsure and unsettled around the house, potentially with me. I am different with him also. I’m back to transactional interactions only, and am being more cautious than before. Now not only do we need to rebuild his trust in me, but I need to rebuild my trust in him.

I don’t blame him for anything, he was doing what he felt he needed to do to protect himself. I don’t blame myself either, I should have thought through what I was doing a bit better, but I can only keep moving forward because 1) Darryl needs me to and 2) there is no going back from here. I can only move forward with this knowledge of what he needs from me and work as hard as possible to be that person until he knows how to ‘dog’ again, and then in return, when he’s able, he’ll be able to be the best dog that I need him to be.

Here’s to always looking forward, thanks for listening.

Elise R

“Optimist: Someone who figures that taking a step backward after taking a step forward is not a disaster, it's a cha-cha.”

― Robert Brault
